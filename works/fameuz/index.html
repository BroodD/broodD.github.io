About course

This online-program is designed to prepare the aspiring screenwriter for writing an original screenplay of feature film and understand how to sell it, by learning what Producers and Studios want, and more importantly, what they do not want.

This course includes 12 step-by-step tutorials and assignments, after completing those you will have created a complete and workable screenplay outline. It's 25% of the entire screenplay creation process.

You will learn and immediately apply new knowledge, and most importantly, you will have the opportunity to receive personal feedback from Ralph DiBacco for each of the 12 completed assignments and your screenplay idea in general.

What You'll
Learn:

  • How to cultivate an original screenplay idea in a logical progression from the firstinspiration to the final outline.
  • How to introduce and develop your protagonist and antagonist
  • How to plan a story using an outline and notecards
  • How to write memorable characters and surprising stories
  • How to recognize and avoid weak and cliché plots
  • How to use misdirection to add conflict for your protagonist
  • How to protect the copyright of your script
  • Which scripts are of interest to modern producers and studios, and how to sell your first script.

Who Should Take This Course:

  • Complete beginner who wants to become a screenwriter
  • Writers who want to understand the structure of feature film
  • Writers who want to understand the functions of each act in a feature film
  • Writers wanting to develop writing skills using targeted creative assignments
  • Writers looking to solidify structure in their screenplays
  • Writers stuck in the second act.
  • Writers seeking a satisfying ending for their screenplays.
  • Writers who wants tips for his/her script from a practicing Hollywood screenwriter.

About your teacher

Ralph W. DiBacco

Hollywood screenwriter and Industry professional with over 30 years of screenwriting and production experience. Teacher in Screenwriters University, Operated By F&W Media.

In 1994, Ralph received certification from Dov S-S Simens’ Hollywood Film Institute, joining such alumni as Queen Latifah, Will Smith, and Quentin Tarantino. That same year, he enrolled in the Hollywood Scriptwriting Institute, where he spent the next two years honing his craft under noted screenwriter Earl Blakesley, Jr., whose screen credits included the popular television shows China Beach and Tour of Duty.

Ralph completed creative writing training through the Christian Writer’s Guild in 1995, the same year that he penned the Mars Hill anthology. He sold his first feature film, Willows Way, to York Entertainment International in 2007, and his second feature film, Back to the Beyond, to Maxim Media International in 2011. He consulted on the psychological thriller Dark Minds, which was sold to R-Squared Films in 2014.

Ralph wrote, produced, and directed ONE:The Motion Picture starring Lance Henriksen (Aliens, The Terminator) and Ian Ziering (Beverly Hills 90210, Sharknado), which has been pre-sold to Adler & Associates Entertainment, Inc. of West Hollywood and will be released in 2019.

Ralph has been teaching aspiring screenwriters since 2013, and has recently received his Master of Fine Arts in Cinema-Television: Script & Screenwriting from Regent University.

Writing the Logline

"The first of 12 tutorials addresses the art and science of the logline, which includes explorations into the composition and application of this brief, but powerful writing tool. Included are examples from world-renowned screenwriting author Blake Snyder, from his groundbreaking book Save The Cat!"

Assignment

Write a logline for your story idea based on the principles explored in this tutorial.

Structure

The second tutorial encompasses the theory of screenwriting structure. Such theorists as Syd Field, Robert McKee, and Linda Seger will be discussed in great detail. A comprehensive 12-point plot summary will be offered to facilitate the outlining process.

Assignment

Create a basic 3-Act outline for your story based on the 12 key plot points explored in this tutorial. (250 words maximum)

Writing The One-Sheet

In this tutorial, we address the importance of writing the one-sheet in the development process. We utilize David Seidler’s masterpiece The King’s Speech in illustrating the dynamics and importance of the one-sheet document.

Assignment

Create a One-Sheet for your screenplay using the principles from Tutorial #1 (Logline) and the 12 key plot points from Tutorial #2 (Story Structure)

Characterization

In this tutorial, we address the importance of character development. We utilize the revolutionary Dramatica Theory of Story in illustrating the eight character archetypes, then examine how these archetypes functioned in such Hollywood films as Star Wars, The Wizard of Oz, and Jaws.

Assignment

Compose a brief biography of your Protagonist or Main Character based on the principles addressed in this tutorial. (250 words maximum)

The Villain

In Tutorial #5 we expand our discussion of Characterization to focus on the one character that is critical to the successful of your screenplay…The Villain. We explore the 15 Villain Archetypes with examples from popular Hollywood films of years past.

Assignment

Compose a brief biography of your Antagonist or Villain using one of the 14 archetypes addressed in this tutorial. (250 words maximum)

Writing The Treatment

In Tutorial #6 we wrap up the first half of our Program with an exploration into Writing the Treatment. We focus on the elements that are inherent in an effective screenplay treatment, and provide examples for the seven plot points/paragraphs that will help your treatment pop off the page!

Assignment

Create a Treatment for your story using the principles addressed in this tutorial. Send in the first 250 words for review.

Genre

In Tutorial #7, we explore the importance of recognizing your film’s genre. The genre encompasses elements that are unique to a particular type of film. Proficiency in mastering these techniques is critical in meeting and exceeding audience expectations.

Assignment

Determine which genre your story encompasses based on John Truby’s list of genres. Record how your story fulfills genre expectations. (250 words maximum)

Writing a Production

Through an exhaustive exploration into the 13 Golden Rules governing the production of low-budget films, we discuss in Tutorial #8 the various ways in which a screenwriter can enhance his or her marketability by creating production-friendly screenplays.

Assignment

Describe how you can make your story more production-friendly based on the 13 ground rules explored in this tutorial. (250 words maximum)

Theme

Having spent considerable time exploring the external forces within the screenwriting process, we shift our focus to the main internal force – theme. In Tutorial #9 we demonstrate how well-crafted thematic elements can enhance both emotion and audience engagement.

Assignment

Describe the thematic elements in your story and how you can make them stronger by using the nine principles presented in this tutorial. (250 words maximum)

Sequences

In Tutorial #10 we shift our focus to an often underutilized writing tool called Sequences. These “mini-movies,” as they are often deemed, are collections of individual scenes that serve to accentuate a particular plot point or direction.

Assignment

List and describe the eight sequences in your story as they adhere to the principles addressed in this tutorial. (250 words maximum)

Crafting the Scene

In Tutorial #11 we examine the two types of scenes that work in conjunction with one another to produce the “heart beat” of your screenplay. The “Scene” encompasses the story’s action, while the “Sequel” encompasses the reactionary elements of both plot and character.

Assignment

List and describe the 10-20 scenes in your story’s Act One as they adhere to the principles addressed in this tutorial. (250 words maximum)

Beats

In our final tutorial of the program, we address the smallest, but most important element of screenplay structure - the beat. Through a careful analysis of such classic films as Star Wars, Terminator, and E.T., we extract the essence of a screenplay’s anatomy through this most powerful component.

Assignment

Revise your Treatment based on the principles addressed in this tutorial. Send in the first 250 words for review.

Feedback from
a practicing screenwriter

The course is divided into 2 packages, a basic (theoretical part of 12 video lessons and 12 tasks to create a plan for your future script) and advanced (checking tasks, getting feedback and recommendations from Ralph Dibacco).
After you complete and upload the task to our website, where the training will take place, teacher will check it and give detailed feedback within 3 days.

Here's what it looks like:

Lexi,

I was impressed with your breakdown of the key story elements. What I found particularly interesting were the parallels that you established between the historical killings and the main characters. For example, the fact that one (or more?) of the female characters are pregnant. This will surely add to viewer engagement.

I also like the introduction of the Professor character. He could be extremely valuable in balancing the “youthful” energy with a more “academic” approach. Besides, you will need a character or two to kill off early. He may fit that need perfectly.

Now, this is just a suggestion, but what if you developed the parallel elements in more detail. What if one of the main characters has blood-ties with one of the killer’s victims? And thus is related to the killer. Because of the parallel structure of the story, the conditions and relations of the characters may be enough to “trigger” a response from the killer’s spirit. Plus, it would make a possession seem more credible, if the character becoming possessed is a descendant of the killer. Just a thought.

I really like the direction that your story is headed. One other character that you may want to consider introducing (if you haven’t done so already) is an older individual who actually knew the family. This would be a great way to reveal backstory and exposition, as it would be organic to the story. Yes, the Professor and library research would serve that purpose. But, how much better would it be to have a first-hand eyewitness! I can’t remember if you mentioned when the original killings took place. But, any time within the past 70 years would be suitable for an eyewitness, albeit an elderly one.

Well Lexi, great job once again. Keep up the great work!
